What is a Brain Tumour?

A brain tumour is an abnormal growth of cells within the brain or central spinal canal. Brain tumours can be benign (non-cancerous) or malignant (cancerous). They can originate in the brain (primary tumours) or spread from other parts of the body (secondary or metastatic tumours).

Important: Not all brain tumours are cancerous. Many brain tumours are benign and can be successfully treated with appropriate medical care.

Types of Brain Tumours

Primary Brain Tumours

  • Gliomas: Arise from glial cells that support nerve cells
    • Astrocytomas (including glioblastoma)
    • Oligodendrogliomas
    • Ependymomas
  • Meningiomas: Develop from the meninges (protective layers around the brain)
  • Pituitary Adenomas: Tumours of the pituitary gland
  • Acoustic Neuromas: Benign tumours affecting hearing and balance nerves
  • Craniopharyngiomas: Rare tumours near the pituitary gland

Secondary (Metastatic) Brain Tumours

These originate from cancers in other parts of the body, commonly from:

  • Lung cancer
  • Breast cancer
  • Kidney cancer
  • Melanoma
  • Colon cancer

Common Symptoms

Brain tumour symptoms vary depending on the tumour's size, type, and location. Common symptoms include:

General Symptoms

  • Persistent headaches that worsen over time
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Seizures (especially new-onset seizures in adults)
  • Changes in vision, hearing, or speech
  • Balance and coordination problems
  • Personality or behaviour changes
  • Memory problems or confusion
  • Weakness or numbness in arms or legs
Seek immediate medical attention if you experience:
  • Sudden, severe headache unlike any you've had before
  • Sudden onset of seizures
  • Sudden vision loss or double vision
  • Sudden weakness or numbness on one side of the body
  • Sudden difficulty speaking or understanding speech

Diagnosis

Diagnosing a brain tumour typically involves several steps:

Medical History and Physical Examination

Your doctor will review your symptoms and perform a neurological examination to assess:

  • Reflexes and muscle strength
  • Coordination and balance
  • Mental status and memory
  • Vision and hearing

Imaging Studies

  • MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging): Provides detailed images of brain tissue
  • CT (Computed Tomography) Scan: Quick imaging to detect tumours and bleeding
  • PET Scan: May help distinguish between tumour types
  • Angiography: Examines blood vessels around the tumour

Biopsy

A tissue sample may be obtained to determine the exact type of tumour and guide treatment decisions.

Treatment Options

Treatment depends on the tumour type, size, location, and your overall health. Options may include:

Surgery

  • Craniotomy: Surgical removal of part or all of the tumour
  • Minimally Invasive Surgery: Using smaller incisions and advanced techniques
  • Image-Guided Surgery: Real-time imaging to precisely locate and remove tumours
  • Awake Craniotomy: For tumours in critical brain areas

Radiation Therapy

  • External beam radiation
  • Stereotactic radiosurgery (Gamma Knife, CyberKnife)
  • Proton beam therapy

Chemotherapy

Medications to destroy cancer cells or slow tumour growth.

Targeted Therapy

Drugs that target specific features of cancer cells.

What to Expect During Treatment

Before Surgery

  • Pre-operative testing and medical clearance
  • Discussion of risks and benefits
  • Preparation instructions
  • Arrangement for post-operative care

After Surgery

  • Recovery in the intensive care unit or neurosurgical ward
  • Monitoring for complications
  • Gradual return to normal activities
  • Follow-up imaging and appointments
  • Possible rehabilitation therapy

Living with a Brain Tumour

Managing Symptoms

  • Take medications as prescribed
  • Attend all follow-up appointments
  • Report new or worsening symptoms immediately
  • Maintain a healthy lifestyle

Support Resources

  • Brain tumour support groups
  • Counselling services
  • Rehabilitation services
  • Social work assistance
  • Palliative care when appropriate

When to Contact Your Doctor

Contact your healthcare team immediately if you experience:

  • New or worsening headaches
  • Seizures
  • Changes in vision or speech
  • Weakness or numbness
  • Confusion or personality changes
  • Persistent nausea or vomiting
  • Signs of infection (fever, wound drainage)
